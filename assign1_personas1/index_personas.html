Some Toasts?

An Investigation into Toaster Interface at Brown Dining Halls

Project Type

Individual Project

Strategies

Research, UI/UX Design

Tools Used

Boords

Duration

2 Weeks

Overview

I was tasked with observing real users interacting with an interface, interviewing these individuals about their experiences, creating personas based on these users, and illustrating a storyboard for one of my personas.

Main Takeaway

It’s important to step into users’ shoes when evaluating an interface. Through this project I found that people’s frustration while using toasters mainly stems from the inability to track the position of the toast. The design of the dial can also be confusing. These results point to possible directions for improvement: a more transparent toasting process and more self-explanatory dials.

Step 1: A sketch and a brief description of the interface

Conveyor toasters use a conveyor to carry bread through a heating chamber where the bread is exposed to heating elements.

Users place their bread onto the conveyor, wait for the toasting process to complete, and retrieve the bread from the tray. The dial can be used to adjust the speed of the conveyor, with slower speeds resulting in darker toast colors. The buttons can be used to choose between top heat or bottom heat, or both.

Step 2: Observations and interviews

I observed several users’ interaction with the toaster and noted some general patterns. Then, I interviewed 3 users to solicit insight about their expectations, experience, motives, and challenges using the interface. My main results are recorded below:

Interview Questions

To better understand the users, I conducted 3 interviews with individuals who use the conveyor toasters at one of Brown's dining halls. Below are the interview questions I designed:

1. How many times in the past week have you used the toaster?

2. What do you usually do when you are waiting for your bread to be toasted?

3. (Follow-up on question 2) Why would you stay by the toaster/walk away/else while you are waiting?

4. What do you think of the speed of the toaster?

5. Have you ever adjusted the speed of the toaster?

Results from Interviews and Observations

  • There are generally two kinds of behavior when people are waiting for their toast. Some of them will leave the toaster and get other food, and return to the toaster after a while to pick up their toast, while others will stand by the toaster and wait.

  • Among those who stand by the toaster and wait, most of them keep staring at the toaster while they are waiting. Most interviewees report a certain degree of impatience at least some of the time when they are waiting for the toast. Not being able to see the toast gives them anxiety. Those who leave the toaster are less conscious of the toasting process.

  • People generally do not adjust the dial before or during the toasting process. Even frequent users of the toaster do not adjust the conveyor speed or other settings. Only in rare cases do they consider changing the settings.

Step 3: Personas

Based on the overarching trends observed amongst the users, I created two personas.

As a regular user of the conveyor toasters, Anna is dissatisfied with the toaster speed. She is particularly annoyed that she can not visually track the location of her toast and is unable to end the toasting process early. Anna represents the group of users who are time-sensitive and care about the quality of their toast.

John represents the group of users who use the toaster without paying attention to the details of its design. He is a casual user who does not care too much about the time it takes or the quality of his toast. John does not have any particular problem with the interface.
