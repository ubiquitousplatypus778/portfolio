CS1300 Iterative Design and Evaluation

Introduction

Homeroom is a startup targeted at young people living in cities that lack affordable housing options by allowing them to rent a single room. They help investors renovate their homes and find qualified renters.


For this assignment, we decided to design a desktop website for the startup. We thought that the website would make it easier for investors to find and connect with the startup, as well as help young people find a room to rent.

During the mockup critique we introduced our Homeroom Concept for 1 minute to briefly, after which our peers interacted with our prototype for 2 minutes. We received feedback for about 10 minutes, which came from the TAs, other students and our industry guest Gifford Cheung, a UX Researcher at Nintendo.


Feedback Summary

  • 188 waterman: More info about property. Given that they are investing, give an option to message between renter and investor.
  • What does “I’m Done” button do - more specification
  • Weird from the investor end of things. One click purchasing is weird for such a large price. Am I forced to pay yet?
  • Cut down on space in favor of information density.
  • Learnability of UI is lacking. Search bar on the first page, what are we searching for? What am I done with? Use space to teach the user.
  • Mobile UI on website. Makes sense that it’s clean - does this fit alongside use-case? More reassurances / explanatory text.
  • Could make it a mobile UI instead that accompanies a complex website.
  • Button labels are very important
  • Both a back button and “X” button on some pages and they have different transitions. Make it consistent.
  • Aligned / justified text (money decimal point) is done well.

3) User Testing

We conducted a remote usability test on three users with the following instructions:


You will be testing an interactive mockup in Figma. Not all functionality for the mockup has been implemented (i.e. buttons may not be clickable). Homeroom is a startup targeted at young people living in cities that lack affordable housing options by allowing them to rent a single room. They also help investors renovate their homes and find qualified renters. We encourage you to think aloud as you perform your tasks!

Tasks

We designed two main tasks for the users:

  • Task 1: You are a renter looking for a room. Find the cheapest room available, add it to your cart, and check out your order.
  • Task 2: You are a wealthy landlord interested in using Homeroom. You have a budget of roughly 1.2 million USD. Find the renovation plan with the largest square footage, and submit a proposal.

Apart from those main tasks, we asked the users to perform four small sub-tasks:

  • Sub-task 1: As a renter, view your profile page.
  • Sub-task 2: As a renter, check out the reviews for a listed room.
  • Sub-task 3: As a renter, click open the filter option.
  • Sub-task 4: As an investor, delete a recommended property.

Reflection on Testers:


  • Tester 1:
    • We were expecting testers to directly follow the main tasks as laid out in the scenario, but our first tester focused heavily on the “side tasks,” ignoring the main instructions. Further, a bunch of problems were caused by them not being able to navigate the overlaying Figma and and UserTesting UIs. For example, one of our side tasks was to click on the “user profile” but they tried to click on the Figma profile page.

  • Tester 2:
    • It took a long time for the tester to click the "match" button. They first wanted to click on the hamburger menu and the filter button, both of which we did not mock up the functionality for. Further, it took some time to use the Homeroom logo as a "home" button. From this, we could change our interface to add in more text that guides the tester even more on what certain buttons do, and what they should be clicking on first.

  • Tester 3:
    • Similarly to the second tester, the third one had trouble figuring out the functionality of different buttons. They also learned to use the HomeRoom icon as a "home button". While both users were eventually able to figure this out, the time it took them to do so suggests that we could make this even more learnable in a further iteration.
