Overview

I worked with three other students to iteratively design a web application for a startup: Homeroom, which helps young people living in cities that lack affordable housing options rent single rooms and helps investors renovate their homes and find qualified renters.

Main Takeaway

People are generally dissatisfied with the toaster interface. The main problem is that they cannot track the position of their toast, which causes impatience and frustration. The design of the dial can also be confusing.

Step 1: Sketching & Wireframing

For this assignment, we decided to design a desktop website for the startup. We thought that the website would make it easier for investors to find and connect with the startup, as well as help young people find a room to rent.

We started by quickly sketching out many different versions of the webpage.

Step 4: Mockup Critique

We presented our prototype in a mockup critique studio, during which we briefly introduced our Homeroom Concept for 1 minute, and our peers interacted with our prototype for 2 minutes. We received feedback for about 10 minutes, which came from the TAs, other students and our industry guest Gifford Cheung, a UX Researcher at Nintendo. We took notes of the feedback and made a final iteration to our prototype.

Feedback Summary

  • Need more info about the properties. Need an option to message between renter and investor.
  • What does “I’m Done” button do - more specification
  • Weird from the investor end of things. One click purchasing is weird for such a large price. Am I forced to pay yet?
  • Cut down on space in favor of information density.
  • Learnability of UI is lacking. Search bar on the first page, what are we searching for? What am I done with? Use space to teach the user.
  • Mobile UI on website. Makes sense that it’s clean - does this fit alongside use-case? More reassurances / explanatory text.
  • Could make it a mobile UI instead that accompanies a complex website.
  • Button labels are very important
  • Both a back button and “X” button on some pages and they have different transitions. Make it consistent.
  • Aligned / justified text (money decimal point) is done well.

Step 5: User Testing

Having test users try out an interface is an important part of testing and a valuable source of feedback. We conducted a remote usability test on three users through a remote user testing service (UserTesting.com)

We provided users with the following instructions:

You will be testing an interactive mockup in Figma. Not all functionality for the mockup has been implemented (i.e. buttons may not be clickable). Homeroom is a startup targeted at young people living in cities that lack affordable housing options by allowing them to rent a single room. They also help investors renovate their homes and find qualified renters. We encourage you to think aloud as you perform your tasks!

We designed two main tasks for the users:

  • Task 1: You are a renter looking for a room. Find the cheapest room available, add it to your cart, and check out your order.
  • Task 2: You are a wealthy landlord interested in using Homeroom. You have a budget of roughly 1.2 million USD. Find the renovation plan with the largest square footage, and submit a proposal.

Apart from those main tasks, we asked the users to perform four small sub-tasks:

  • Sub-task 1: As a renter, view your profile page.
  • Sub-task 2: As a renter, check out the reviews for a listed room.
  • Sub-task 3: As a renter, click open the filter option.
  • Sub-task 4: As an investor, delete a recommended property.

Usability

The long lists of menu items make it hard for users to locate items. Also, the food items are not listed in any particular order. The layout of buttons and categories is also quite random, which hinders usability.

Memorability

The webpage is not memorable as a homepage: there are no pictures, only large blocks of text. It doesn’t even show the name of the bakery.

Learnability

The repetition of items such as “Cake” and "Breakfast" in both the navigation bar and the text creates confusion. Also, there are two buttons: “ORDER CAKES ONLINE” and “ONLINE STORE” that lead to the same page, which decreases learnability.
